# Transfer Pricing

## Scope and the arm's length principle

Indian transfer pricing under Sections 92 to 92F applies to international transactions between associated enterprises (26% voting power, common control, or specified relationships) and to specified domestic transactions above ₹20 crore. Every covered transaction must be priced at the arm's length price (ALP) — the price unrelated parties would agree in comparable circumstances.

## Methods

The prescribed methods are: Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP), Resale Price Method, Cost Plus Method, Profit Split Method, Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM), and any Other Method (Rule 10AB). TNMM is the most used in practice because reliable net-margin comparables are easier to find than transaction-level prices. Where results fall within the arm's length range (35th to 65th percentile of the comparable set, when six or more comparables exist), no adjustment is made; otherwise the median applies.

## Documentation and filings

Taxpayers with international transactions above ₹1 crore must maintain contemporaneous documentation under Rule 10D: entity overview, industry analysis, functional analysis (functions, assets, risks), method selection and benchmarking. Form 3CEB, certified by a chartered accountant, is due by 31 October. The three-tier BEPS regime adds the Master File (Form 3CEAA, group revenue above ₹500 crore) and Country-by-Country Report (Form 3CEAD, consolidated group revenue above ₹6,400 crore).

## Penalties and dispute management

Missing documentation attracts a penalty of 2% of transaction value; non-filing of Form 3CEB costs ₹1,00,000. TP adjustments commonly arise on management fees, royalty payments, intra-group financing and share issuance. Dispute options include Dispute Resolution Panel (DRP) within 30 days of the draft order, appeals, unilateral or bilateral Advance Pricing Agreements (APAs valid up to five years with four-year rollback), and Safe Harbour rules for eligible IT/ITES and KPO services with prescribed margins.

## Practical risk indicators

Persistent losses in a captive service provider, royalty above industry norms, unbenchmarked cost allocations, and interest-free loans to subsidiaries are the classic audit triggers. Aligning intercompany agreements, invoicing and actual conduct is the first line of defence.
